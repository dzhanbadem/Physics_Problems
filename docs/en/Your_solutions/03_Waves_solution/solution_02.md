For this problem, we look at **standing waves** on a string. Since the string is fixed at both ends, it creates a specific pattern of vibration.

## 2. String Harmonics: The Guitar String

### The Concept
When a string is plucked, it vibrates at its **fundamental frequency** (also called the first harmonic). In this state, the string forms a single "loop" or **antinode** in the middle, with **nodes** (points of no movement) at each fixed end.

The key insight for your presentation is the relationship between the length of the string ($L$) and the wavelength ($\lambda$). For the fundamental frequency:
$$L = \frac{1}{2}\lambda \quad \text{or} \quad \lambda = 2L$$



---

### The Setup
* **Length of string ($L$):** $64\text{ cm} = 0.64\text{ m}$ (Always convert to SI units!)
* **Fundamental Frequency ($f_1$):** $330\text{ Hz}$
* **Target:** Wave speed ($v$)

---

### Step-by-Step Execution

**1. Determine the Wavelength ($\lambda$):**
Since the fundamental frequency consists of exactly half a wavelength stretched across the string:
$$\lambda = 2 \times L$$
$$\lambda = 2 \times 0.64\text{ m}$$
$$\lambda = 1.28\text{ m}$$

**2. Calculate the Wave Speed ($v$):**
Now, use the universal wave equation:
$$v = f \lambda$$
Substitute the known values:
$$v = 330\text{ Hz} \times 1.28\text{ m}$$
$$v = 422.4\text{ m/s}$$

---

### Summary for Presentation
* **Wavelength:** $1.28\text{ m}$
* **Wave Speed:** $422.4\text{ m/s}$

> **Pro-Tip for Class:** Explain that the speed of a wave on a string is determined by the **tension** and the **linear density** (mass per unit length) of the string. This is why tuning a guitar by tightening the knob (increasing tension) changes the pitch—it increases the wave speed, which in turn raises the frequency since the string length remains the same!
