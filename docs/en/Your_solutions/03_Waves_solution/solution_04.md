This question explores the relationship between physical distance and the "stage" of a wave's cycle, which we call **phase**.

## 4. Phase Difference

### The Concept
A wave is a periodic phenomenon. One full cycle of a wave covers a physical distance of one wavelength ($\lambda$) and corresponds to a full circular rotation of $2\pi$ radians (or $360^\circ$). 

Phase difference ($\phi$) tells us how far "out of sync" two points are. If the distance between two points is a fraction of the wavelength, the phase difference will be that same fraction of $2\pi$.



---

### The Setup
* **Separation distance ($\Delta x$):** $\frac{\lambda}{3}$
* **Full cycle distance:** $\lambda$
* **Full cycle phase:** $2\pi$ radians
* **Target:** Phase difference ($\Delta \phi$)

---

### Step-by-Step Execution

**1. Use the Ratio Method**
The simplest way to visualize this is by setting up a ratio. The ratio of the distance to the wavelength is equal to the ratio of the phase difference to a full circle:
$$\frac{\Delta \phi}{2\pi} = \frac{\Delta x}{\lambda}$$

**2. Substitute the given distance**
Replace $\Delta x$ with the value provided in the problem ($\lambda/3$):
$$\frac{\Delta \phi}{2\pi} = \frac{\lambda/3}{\lambda}$$

**3. Simplify the expression**
The $\lambda$ terms cancel out:
$$\frac{\Delta \phi}{2\pi} = \frac{1}{3}$$

**4. Solve for $\Delta \phi$**
Multiply both sides by $2\pi$:
$$\Delta \phi = \frac{2\pi}{3} \text{ radians}$$

---

### Summary for Presentation
* **Phase Difference:** $\frac{2\pi}{3}$ radians (which is $120^\circ$).

> **Pro-Tip for Class:** To make this intuitive, tell your classmates to think of the wave as a clock. A full wavelength is a full 12-hour rotation ($2\pi$). Moving a distance of $\lambda/3$ is like moving the clock hand $1/3$ of the way around the circle—from 12 o'clock to 4 o'clock!
