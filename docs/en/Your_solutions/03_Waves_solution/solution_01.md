For this first problem, we are looking at how a wave’s physical properties change—or stay the same—when it moves through different media.

## 1. Wave Properties: Sound in Air vs. Water

### The Concept
The fundamental relationship for any wave is the **Wave Equation**. This equation links the speed of the wave to its frequency and wavelength. 

A critical point to remember for your presentation: **Frequency is determined by the source.** When a sound wave moves from air to water, the frequency ($f$) stays the same ($440\text{ Hz}$), but the speed ($v$) and wavelength ($\lambda$) change.



---

### Part A: Wavelength in Air
**The Setup:**
* **Frequency ($f$):** $440\text{ Hz}$
* **Speed in Air ($v_{air}$):** $343\text{ m/s}$
* **Target:** Wavelength ($\lambda_{air}$)

**Step-by-Step Execution:**
1.  **State the formula:**
    $$v = f \lambda$$
2.  **Rearrange to solve for wavelength ($\lambda$):**
    $$\lambda = \frac{v}{f}$$
3.  **Substitute the values:**
    $$\lambda_{air} = \frac{343\text{ m/s}}{440\text{ Hz}}$$
4.  **Calculate:**
    $$\lambda_{air} \approx 0.78\text{ m}$$

---

### Part B: Wavelength in Water
**The Setup:**
* **Frequency ($f$):** $440\text{ Hz}$ (Remains constant)
* **Speed in Water ($v_{water}$):** $1482\text{ m/s}$
* **Target:** Wavelength ($\lambda_{water}$)

**Step-by-Step Execution:**
1.  **Use the same rearranged formula:**
    $$\lambda_{water} = \frac{v_{water}}{f}$$
2.  **Substitute the new speed:**
    $$\lambda_{water} = \frac{1482\text{ m/s}}{440\text{ Hz}}$$
3.  **Calculate:**
    $$\lambda_{water} \approx 3.37\text{ m}$$

---

### Summary Table for Presentation
| Medium | Speed ($v$) | Frequency ($f$) | Wavelength ($\lambda$) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Air** | $343\text{ m/s}$ | $440\text{ Hz}$ | $0.78\text{ m}$ |
| **Water** | $1482\text{ m/s}$ | $440\text{ Hz}$ | $3.37\text{ m}$ |

> **Pro-Tip for Class:** Point out that because water is much denser and less compressible than air, sound travels over 4 times faster in it. Consequently, the wavelength must stretch significantly to "keep up" with that higher speed at the same frequency.
