This problem combines two major concepts: **Energy Conservation** (to find how fast the electron is moving) and **Magnetic Force** (to find the radius of the circular path).

---

## Problem Breakdown: Cyclotron Motion

### 1. The Concept
*   **Step A:** When an electron is accelerated through a potential difference ($V$), its electrical potential energy is converted into kinetic energy.
*   **Step B:** When a moving charge enters a magnetic field ($B$) perpendicularly, the magnetic force acts as a **centripetal force**, pulling the electron into a perfect circle.



### 2. Step A: Finding the Velocity ($v$)
The kinetic energy ($K$) gained is equal to the charge multiplied by the potential difference ($qV$).
$$\frac{1}{2}mv^2 = qV$$

Solving for $v$:
$$v = \sqrt{\frac{2qV}{m}}$$

**The Variables:**
*   $q = 1.602 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}$
*   $V = 5000 \text{ V}$
*   $m = 9.11 \times 10^{-31} \text{ kg}$

$$v = \sqrt{\frac{2 \cdot (1.602 \times 10^{-19}) \cdot 5000}{9.11 \times 10^{-31}}} \approx 4.19 \times 10^7 \text{ m/s}$$
*(Note: This is about 14% the speed of light, so we don't need to worry about relativity yet.)*

### 3. Step B: Finding the Radius ($r$)
In a magnetic field, the magnetic force ($F_B = qvB$) provides the centripetal force ($F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r}$).
$$qvB = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$

Rearranging to solve for the radius ($r$):
$$r = \frac{mv}{qB}$$

**Plugging in our values:**
*   $m = 9.11 \times 10^{-31} \text{ kg}$
*   $v = 4.19 \times 10^7 \text{ m/s}$
*   $q = 1.602 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}$
*   $B = 0.1 \text{ T}$

$$r = \frac{(9.11 \times 10^{-31}) \cdot (4.19 \times 10^7)}{(1.602 \times 10^{-19}) \cdot 0.1}$$
$$r \approx 0.00238 \text{ m}$$

### 4. Final Result
*   **Radius:** $2.38 \text{ mm}$

### Logic Check for your Class:
Explain that the **mass** of an electron is incredibly small, which is why the radius is so tiny (just a few millimeters) despite the electron moving at millions of meters per second. If we used a proton instead, which is much heavier, the radius would be significantly larger for the same field.

**Key insight:** This principle is exactly how mass spectrometers work—they use magnetic fields to "bend" atoms of different masses into different sized circles to identify them!
