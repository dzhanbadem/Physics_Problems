This problem focuses on the **Lorentz Force**, which is the force exerted on a charged particle moving through magnetic and electric fields. In this specific case, there is no electric field mentioned, so we are calculating the magnetic component of that force.

---

## Problem Breakdown: Lorentz Force

### 1. The Concept
The general Lorentz Force equation is:
$$\vec{F} = q(\vec{E} + \vec{v} \times \vec{B})$$

Since the problem does not mention an electric field ($E = 0$), the formula simplifies to the magnetic force:
$$F_B = qvB \sin(\theta)$$
*   **$q$** is the charge.
*   **$v$** is the velocity.
*   **$B$** is the magnetic field strength.
*   **$\theta$** is the angle between the velocity and the magnetic field.



### 2. The Variables
From the problem description, we have:
*   **Charge ($q$):** $2 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}$
*   **Mass ($m$):** $4 \times 10^{-27} \text{ kg}$ (Note: Mass is given, but it is not needed to find the *force*, only the resulting acceleration.)
*   **Magnetic Field ($B$):** $0.5 \text{ T}$
*   **Velocity ($v$):** $10^6 \text{ m/s}$
*   **Angle ($\theta$):** $90^\circ$ (Because the particle enters "perpendicular to the field").

### 3. Step-by-Step Calculation
Since the movement is perpendicular, $\sin(90^\circ) = 1$. The formula becomes:
$$F = qvB$$

Now, plug in the values:
$$F = (2 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}) \cdot (10^6 \text{ m/s}) \cdot (0.5 \text{ T})$$

Multiply the numbers:
$$F = (2 \cdot 10^6 \cdot 0.5) \times 10^{-19}$$
$$F = (1.0) \times 10^{-13} \text{ N}$$

### 4. Final Result
*   **Magnitude of Lorentz Force:** $1.0 \times 10^{-13} \text{ N}$

---

### Logic Check for your Class:
A force of $10^{-13} \text{ N}$ might sound tiny, but remember the **mass** of the particle ($4 \times 10^{-27} \text{ kg}$). Using Newton's Second Law ($a = F/m$):
$$a = \frac{10^{-13}}{4 \times 10^{-27}} = 2.5 \times 10^{13} \text{ m/s}^2$$

This highlights a key theme in your presentation: small forces can cause **massive accelerations** when dealing with subatomic particles.

**Direction Note:** While the question only asks for magnitude, you can mention the **Right-Hand Rule** to your class. If they point their thumb in the direction of velocity and fingers in the direction of the magnetic field, their palm shows the direction of the force.
