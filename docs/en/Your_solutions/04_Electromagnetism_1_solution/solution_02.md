Calculating **Electric Potential ($V$)** is often much simpler than calculating Electric Force because potential is a **scalar quantity**. This means we don't need to worry about vectors or directions; we simply calculate the value for each charge and add them all together algebraically.

---

## Problem Breakdown: Potential at the Center

### 1. The Concept
The electric potential ($V$) created by a point charge at a distance ($r$) is given by:
$$V = k \frac{q}{r}$$
Where $k$ is Coulomb's constant ($8.99 \times 10^9 \text{ N}\cdot\text{m}^2/\text{C}^2$). Because potential is a scalar, the total potential at the center ($V_{total}$) is the sum of the potentials from each individual charge:
$$V_{total} = V_1 + V_2 + V_3 + V_4$$

### 2. The Geometry
As established in the previous problem, the distance ($r$) from the corner of a 1.0 m square to its center is:
$$r = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \approx 0.707 \text{ m}$$
Since all charges are at the corners, $r$ is the **same** for all four charges.



### 3. The Calculation
We can factor out the common terms ($k$ and $r$) to make the math cleaner:
$$V_{total} = \frac{k}{r} (q_1 + q_2 + q_3 + q_4)$$

**Plugging in the values:**
*   $q_1 = +1.0 \text{ C}$
*   $q_2 = -2.0 \text{ C}$
*   $q_3 = +3.0 \text{ C}$
*   $q_4 = -4.0 \text{ C}$

**Sum of charges:**
$$\sum q = 1.0 - 2.0 + 3.0 - 4.0 = -2.0 \text{ C}$$

**Final Computation:**
$$V_{total} = \frac{8.99 \times 10^9}{0.707} \times (-2.0)$$
$$V_{total} \approx (1.27 \times 10^{10}) \times (-2.0)$$
$$V_{total} \approx -2.54 \times 10^{10} \text{ Volts}$$

### 4. Final Result
*   **Electric Potential:** $-2.54 \times 10^{10} \text{ V}$ (or $-25.4 \text{ GV}$)

### Key Learning Points for your Presentation:
*   **Scalar vs. Vector:** Remind the class that while Force (from the last problem) required looking at angles and cancellation, Potential only requires simple addition.
*   **Sign Matters:** In potential problems, you **must** include the negative signs for the charges. A negative potential means a positive test charge would be "trapped" in a potential well at that point.
*   **Superposition:** This is a perfect example of the Principle of Superposition—the total effect is just the sum of the individual parts.
