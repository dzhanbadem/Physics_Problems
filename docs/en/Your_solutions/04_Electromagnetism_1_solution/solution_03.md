To solve for the **equilibrium position**, we need to find the specific point where the net force acting on the third charge is zero. In other words, the "push" from the left must perfectly balance the "push" from the right.

---

## Problem Breakdown: Electrostatic Equilibrium

### 1. The Concept
Equilibrium occurs when the vector sum of the forces is zero ($\sum F = 0$). Since $q_1$ and $q_2$ are both positive, they will both repel the positive charge $q_3$. 
*   $q_1$ pushes $q_3$ to the **right**.
*   $q_2$ pushes $q_3$ to the **left**.
At the equilibrium point, these two forces must have equal magnitudes: $F_1 = F_2$.

### 2. Setting Up the Variables
Let's place $q_1$ at the origin ($x = 0$).
*   Charge $q_1 = +4\text{ C}$ is at $x = 0$.
*   Charge $q_2 = +9\text{ C}$ is at $x = 2.0\text{ m}$.
*   Let the position of $q_3$ be **$x$**.
*   The distance from $q_1$ to $q_3$ is **$x$**.
*   The distance from $q_2$ to $q_3$ is **$(2 - x)$**.



### 3. The Formula
Using Coulomb's Law ($F = k \frac{q_a q_b}{r^2}$), we set the force from $q_1$ equal to the force from $q_2$:

$$k \frac{q_1 q_3}{x^2} = k \frac{q_2 q_3}{(2 - x)^2}$$

### 4. Step-by-Step Simplification
Notice that $k$ and $q_3$ appear on both sides, so they cancel out. This tells us that the equilibrium position **does not depend** on the magnitude or sign of the third charge!

$$\frac{4}{x^2} = \frac{9}{(2 - x)^2}$$

To solve this easily without a quadratic equation, take the **square root** of both sides:

$$\sqrt{\frac{4}{x^2}} = \sqrt{\frac{9}{(2 - x)^2}}$$
$$\frac{2}{x} = \frac{3}{2 - x}$$

Now, cross-multiply:
$$2(2 - x) = 3x$$
$$4 - 2x = 3x$$
$$4 = 5x$$
$$x = 0.8\text{ m}$$

### 5. Final Result
*   **Position:** $0.8\text{ m}$ from the $+4\text{C}$ charge (or $1.2\text{ m}$ from the $+9\text{C}$ charge).

---

### Logic Check for your Class:
Ask your students: "Should the equilibrium point be closer to the $4\text{C}$ charge or the $9\text{C}$ charge?"
Since the $9\text{C}$ charge is much stronger, the equilibrium point *must* be further away from it and closer to the weaker $4\text{C}$ charge to balance the forces. Our result ($0.8\text{m}$ vs $1.2\text{m}$) confirms this intuition.
