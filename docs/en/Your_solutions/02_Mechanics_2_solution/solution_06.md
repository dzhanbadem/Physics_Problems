This problem explores how energy loss affects the physical behavior of an object. In this case, the "lost" energy usually turns into heat and sound during the impact with the floor.

### 1. The "Given" & "Find"
* **Initial Height ($h_0$):** $2.0$ m
* **Energy Loss per bounce:** $30\%$ (which means $70\%$ or $0.7$ of the energy is **retained**)
* **Find:** Height after the second bounce ($h_2$)

---

### 2. The Concept
The gravitational potential energy ($PE$) of the ball at its peak height is given by:
$$PE = mgh$$
Because $m$ and $g$ are constants, the energy is **directly proportional** to the height ($PE \propto h$). This means if the ball loses $30\%$ of its energy, it will also lose $30\%$ of its height compared to the previous bounce.



---

### 3. The Setup
If the ball retains $70\%$ ($0.7$) of its energy, the height after any bounce can be calculated by:
$$h_{new} = h_{old} \times 0.7$$

To find the height after the **second** bounce, we apply this factor twice:
$$h_2 = h_0 \times 0.7 \times 0.7$$
Or:
$$h_2 = h_0 \times (0.7)^2$$

---

### 4. The Calculation
**Step 1: Calculate the height after the first bounce ($h_1$).**
$$h_1 = 2.0\text{ m} \times 0.7 = 1.4\text{ m}$$

**Step 2: Calculate the height after the second bounce ($h_2$).**
$$h_2 = 1.4\text{ m} \times 0.7$$
$$h_2 = 0.98\text{ m}$$

---

### 5. The Result
The tennis ball will rise to a height of **$0.98$ meters** (or $98$ cm) after the second bounce.

**Teaching Tip:** You can show your class that for any number of bounces ($n$), the formula is $h_n = h_0 \times (r)^n$, where $r$ is the retention rate. This is a great way to link physics with geometric sequences in math!
