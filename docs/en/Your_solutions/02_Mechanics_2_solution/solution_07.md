This is a great problem for your class because it requires looking at "layers" of friction. Since the top block is tied to the wall, it doesn't move, but it still exerts a frictional force on the bottom block as it slides beneath it.

### 1. The "Given" & "Find"
* **Mass of top block ($m_1$):** $5\text{ kg}$ (Stationary, tied to wall)
* **Mass of bottom block ($m_2$):** $10\text{ kg}$
* **Applied Force ($F_{app}$):** $45\text{ N}$
* **Coefficient of kinetic friction ($\mu_k$):** $0.2$
* **Find:** Acceleration of the $10\text{ kg}$ block ($a_2$)

---

### 2. The Concept
To find the acceleration, we use **Newton's Second Law**: $F_{net} = ma$. 
The $10\text{ kg}$ block faces two opposing frictional forces:
1.  **$f_1$ (Top surface):** Friction between the $5\text{ kg}$ block and the $10\text{ kg}$ block.
2.  **$f_2$ (Bottom surface):** Friction between the $10\text{ kg}$ block and the floor.



---

### 3. The Setup: Calculating Frictional Forces
Friction is calculated as $f = \mu_k \times N$, where $N$ is the Normal Force.

**Step 1: Friction from the top block ($f_1$)**
The Normal force here is just the weight of the $5\text{ kg}$ block.
$$N_1 = m_1 g = 5 \times 9.8 = 49\text{ N}$$
$$f_1 = \mu_k N_1 = 0.2 \times 49 = 9.8\text{ N}$$

**Step 2: Friction from the floor ($f_2$)**
The floor must support **both** blocks. Therefore, the Normal force is the total weight.
$$N_2 = (m_1 + m_2)g = (5 + 10) \times 9.8 = 15 \times 9.8 = 147\text{ N}$$
$$f_2 = \mu_k N_2 = 0.2 \times 147 = 29.4\text{ N}$$

---

### 4. The Calculation
Now we apply the equation for the $10\text{ kg}$ block:
$$F_{net} = F_{app} - f_1 - f_2$$
$$m_2 a_2 = 45 - 9.8 - 29.4$$

**Step 3: Solve for $a_2$.**
$$10 \times a_2 = 5.8$$
$$a_2 = \frac{5.8}{10}$$
$$a_2 = 0.58\text{ m/s}^2$$

---

### 5. The Result
The acceleration of the $10\text{ kg}$ block is **$0.58\text{ m/s}^2$**.

**Teaching Tip:** Emphasize to the class that the $5\text{ kg}$ block acts like a "brake" on top of the moving block. Even though it's not moving, its weight creates friction that the $10\text{ kg}$ block must overcome. Also, notice we only used $m_2$ (the $10\text{ kg}$ block) for the final $F=ma$ calculation because it is the only object actually accelerating!
