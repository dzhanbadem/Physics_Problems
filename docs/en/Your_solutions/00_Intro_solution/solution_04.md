To rearrange the formula for the acceleration due to gravity ($g$), we follow a logical sequence of algebraic steps to isolate $g$ on one side of the equation.

### 1. The Original Formula
The period ($T$) of a simple pendulum depends on its length ($L$) and the local acceleration due to gravity ($g$):
$$T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}$$



---

### 2. Step-by-Step Rearrangement

**Step A: Isolate the square root**
Divide both sides of the equation by $2\pi$:
$$\frac{T}{2\pi} = \sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}$$

**Step B: Remove the square root**
Square both sides of the equation to eliminate the radical:
$$\left( \frac{T}{2\pi} \right)^2 = \frac{L}{g}$$
$$\frac{T^2}{4\pi^2} = \frac{L}{g}$$

**Step C: Isolate $g$**
First, multiply both sides by $g$ to move it out of the denominator:
$$g \cdot \frac{T^2}{4\pi^2} = L$$

Now, multiply both sides by the reciprocal of the fraction ($\frac{4\pi^2}{T^2}$) to isolate $g$:
$$g = \frac{4\pi^2 L}{T^2}$$

---

### 3. Final Result
The rearranged formula to calculate the acceleration due to gravity is:
$$\mathbf{g = \frac{4\pi^2 L}{T^2}}$$

### 4. Physical Interpretation
* **Direct Proportionality:** The value of $g$ is directly proportional to the length ($L$) of the pendulum.
* **Inverse Proportionality:** The value of $g$ is inversely proportional to the square of the period ($T^2$). This means if the period increases (the pendulum swings slower), the gravity must be weaker.

**Experimental Note:** This rearranged formula is commonly used in physics labs to experimentally determine the value of Earth's gravity by measuring the length of a string and the time it takes for the pendulum to complete several oscillations.
