To solve this system of linear equations, we can use either the **Substitution Method** or the **Elimination Method**. I will demonstrate the Elimination Method as it is often the most direct.

### 1. The Given Equations
We start with two linear equations:
1.  $2x + 3y = 12$
2.  $x - y = 1$



---

### 2. Step-by-Step Calculation (Elimination Method)

**Step A: Align the variables**
To eliminate $y$, we need the coefficients of $y$ in both equations to be opposites. We can multiply the second equation by $3$.
* Equation 1: $2x + 3y = 12$
* Equation 2 (Multiplied by 3): $3(x - y) = 3(1) \implies 3x - 3y = 3$

**Step B: Add the equations together**
Now, add the two equations to cancel out $y$:
$$(2x + 3y) + (3x - 3y) = 12 + 3$$
$$5x = 15$$

**Step C: Solve for $x$**
Divide both sides by $5$:
$$x = \frac{15}{5} = \mathbf{3}$$

**Step D: Substitute $x$ back to find $y$**
Use the simplest original equation ($x - y = 1$) and plug in $x = 3$:
$$3 - y = 1$$
$$-y = 1 - 3$$
$$-y = -2 \implies y = \mathbf{2}$$

---

### 3. Verification
Plug $x = 3$ and $y = 2$ back into the first equation to check for accuracy:
$$2(3) + 3(2) = 12$$
$$6 + 6 = 12$$
**$12 = 12$** (The solution is correct!)

---

### 4. Interpretation and Context
In a geometric sense, each of these equations represents a straight line on a graph. The values $x = 3$ and $y = 2$ represent the **point of intersection** $(3, 2)$ where both lines cross.
